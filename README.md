Comparison of antimicrobial susceptibility testing systems VITEK REVEAL
and BD Phoenix for Gram-negative bacteraemia

03. Bacterial susceptibility & resistance | 03c. Susceptibility testing methods (incl assay validation, phenotypic assays and
comparative studies, excl TB)

J. Tat¹, H. Glassman¹, C. Fletcher¹, S. Feng¹, A. Zhang¹, P. Parekh¹, V. Tang¹, M. Charles¹. 
¹Vancouver Coastal Health – Vancouver (Canada)

Background

Timely administration of effective antibiotic therapy is critical to patient outcomes in bloodstream infections. Conventional and
automated phenotypic antimicrobial susceptibility testing (AST) methods are culture-dependent and typically require subculture,
delaying results. The rapid AST system VITEK® REVEAL™ provides phenotypic susceptibility results directly from positive blood
cultures within hours rather than days after culture positivity, enabling earlier initiation of targeted antibiotic therapy.

Methods

During summer 2025, prospective positive blood cultures identified as monomicrobial Gram-negative organisms on Gram stain
were enrolled. The local routine workflow of organism identification using the Bruker MALDI Biotyper® followed by AST using BD
Phoenix™ after further incubation (smudge technique) was compared with direct inoculation of positive blood cultures onto the
VITEK® REVEAL™ system. Cultured isolates were identified by MALDI, and organism identification was entered into REVEAL™
for susceptibility interpretation.

Results

A total of 113 GN-positive blood cultures were enrolled, of which 14 were excluded for being polymicrobial, having species not claimed on the Reveal and technical and system errors. Overall, 98% of isolates were Enterobacterales, 69% were Escherichia coli. The Reveal™ demonstrated 76.7% essential agreement (EA) and 97.5% categorical agreement (CA) for the covered organism-antibiotic combinations. Lower than expected EA is likely attributed to limited breakpoint coverage compared to Phoenix™ for multiple antibiotics. There was a very major error (VME) rate of 2.2%, which was principally attributed to ESBL-producing organisms and cephalosporins, a major error (ME) rate of 0.2% and a minor error rate of 2.1%. Adjudication with gradient diffusion strip testing (E-test) resolved one minor error (mE) in favour of Reveal™ and broth microdilution testing resolved 1 VME, 2 ME and 1 mE. 24% of isolates exhibited an extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL) phenotype, with 98% CA. Median time to result (from positivity to complete AST) for Reveal™ was 8 hours (range 6-8.2h), with results available approximately a day earlier compared to routine practice.

Conclusions

The VITEK® REVEAL™ system provides rapid, actionable AST results with high categorical agreement compared with
standard-of-care testing, supporting earlier targeted antimicrobial therapy. However, selected organism–antibiotic combinations,
particularly those involving ESBL-producing organisms, may warrant confirmatory testing.
